PROBLEM 1 Lower Effective Rates

The sector picture: CMHCs collect less per claim than non-CMHC providers on most service codes. The total sector rate gap: $8.1M.

The benchmark: Rest-of-FL P75 — best-in-class. This is the transformation target. Every comparison below is against P75. The question is simple: are you operating at best-in-class? If not, how big is the gap?

PROBLEM 3 Clinician Churn and Burnout

The sector picture: CMHCs carry the sector's most complex patients at the lowest effective rates — a combination that compresses clinician productivity and contributes to burnout. When rates are lower, clinicians must see more patients to generate the same revenue. When patients are higher-acuity, each visit takes more clinical time. The result: burnout that drives churn, which further constrains capacity.

Panel sizes below are benchmarked against Rest-of-FL market norms. Henderson's therapy and PSR panels are approximately at market. But case management and assessment panels are genuinely extreme — and they connect directly to the below-best-in-class rates in Problem 1.

Where you sit: Two codes at market, two codes extreme

Combined with a rate that is -43.1% below best-in-class and falling, this is the burnout cycle at full throttle: clinicians must see more patients because each patient generates less revenue.

Question: What is the turnover rate among CM clinicians? At 595.6 benes per clinician, is this sustainable — or are clinicians triaging rather than engaging? Does the high panel explain the low RPB ($226.58 vs $370.51 best-in-class)?

H0031: Extreme throughput — 2.6x the market

Assessment throughput of 200.6 benes per clinician is +162% above market (76.5). Five clinicians are processing an enormous volume of assessments. This could explain the very low per-claim rate ($21.40 vs $50.77 best-in-class): if assessments are brief to handle volume, fewer units are billed per claim, depressing the effective rate.

Question: Are assessments being truncated to manage throughput? Is the -57.8% rate gap a direct consequence of the extreme panel size?

The reframing: H2019 and H2017 are at market

Henderson's therapy panels (222.2 vs 227.0 market) and PSR panels (245.5 vs 260.0) are approximately at market. These lines are not the burnout concern. The pressure is concentrated entirely in case management and assessment.

Investigation Priorities

1. T1017 Rate + Caseload Crisis

The rate is -43.1% below best-in-class ($44.86 vs $78.88) and the trend is falling. Panels are 134% above market (595.6 vs 254.11). RPB is -38.8% below best-in-class. These three metrics form a reinforcing loop: below-best-in-class rates force high volume, high volume compresses engagement, compressed engagement depresses RPB. Investigate denial rates, modifier optimization, unit billing practices, and contract terms on T1017.

Impact: T1017 is 16.2% of revenue (~$0.53M). Closing the rate gap to best-in-class on current volume would add ~$0.49M.

2. H2017 Rate Recovery Acceleration

PSR at $98.96 is -26.7% below best-in-class ($134.97). The trend is recovering — something changed recently. At 32.5% of revenue (~$1.07M), this is Henderson's second-largest line. Identify what drove the recovery and determine if it can be accelerated toward best-in-class.

Impact: Closing the gap to best-in-class on current volume would add significant revenue. Panels are at market (245.5 vs 260.0), so the lever here is rate improvement, not volume.

3. H0031 Assessment Throughput vs Quality

Assessment panels at 200.6 are 162% above market (76.5). The rate is -57.8% below best-in-class. These two facts may be linked: extreme throughput may produce briefer assessments billed at fewer units, depressing the per-claim rate. Investigate whether assessment quality and depth are being sacrificed for volume.

Impact: Small direct revenue (1.8%), but assessment quality drives downstream engagement. If brief assessments lead to poor care matching, the ripple effects reach therapy and CM retention.

4. H2019 Therapy: Protect and Replicate

Henderson's therapy rate of $79.53 is the closest to best-in-class (-3.8%) and RPB is +26.8% above best-in-class. Panels are at market (222.2 vs 227.0). This line is operating well — protect it. The question: can the billing practices, contract terms, or care model that produce this near-best-in-class performance be applied to other lines, particularly T1017 and H2017?

Impact: This is Henderson's strongest line. Losing the rate or engagement advantage here would be more damaging than gains elsewhere.

Henderson's Story

Henderson has the volume. The patients are there. But every major code is below best-in-class on rates, and the caseloads to compensate are extreme. Case management is under severe strain: 5 clinicians carry nearly 3,000 patients at a rate that is -43.1% below best-in-class and declining year over year. Assessment throughput is 162% above market, likely compressing assessment quality and the rates that follow.

The bright spot is behavioral therapy (H2019): near-best-in-class rates, above-best-in-class RPB, and market-appropriate panels. Henderson has demonstrated it can operate at or near the top quartile on one code. The transformation path is clear: close the rate gaps on T1017 and H2017 so clinicians don't have to carry unsustainable panels to compensate.

Methodology

Data source: FL Medicaid FFS claims (DOGE/Medicaid Provider Spending), 2024. MCO encounter data not included.

Org identification: Claims matched by billing NPI to organizational entity via NPPES. Professional servicing NPIs only (entity_type_code = 1).

Benchmark philosophy: This report benchmarks against Rest-of-FL P75 — best-in-class. This is the top quartile of non-CMHC FL Medicaid providers. The CMHC sector median is shown in small gray text for context only — it is never the comparison. The rationale: the industry landscape report demonstrated that the CMHC sector systematically underperforms the broader market. Benchmarking against the sector normalizes that underperformance. Best-in-class is the transformation target.

Peer groups: Rest-of-FL P75 (top quartile, excludes CMHCs — the benchmark). CMHC sector median (86 orgs, context only, shown in gray).

Metrics: Payment per claim (rate), revenue per beneficiary (engagement depth), claims per beneficiary (visit frequency), beneficiaries per clinician (panel size / productivity).

Sector context: Sector-level numbers ($8.1M rate gap, 23.6%→5.0% leakage, $157M upper-bound leakage gap) are from the FL CMHC Landscape Report v4, which benchmarks the full CMHC sector against all non-CMHC FL Medicaid providers.

Limitations: This report shows FL Medicaid FFS only — a partial view. Organization-level conclusions should be validated against internal data. MCO encounter data, commercial payers, grants, and other funding sources are not captured. Beneficiary counts are not deduplicated across service codes. Revenue breakdown by service line is approximate where exact code-level splits were unavailable.
