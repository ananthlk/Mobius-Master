PROBLEM 3 Clinician Churn and Burnout

The sector picture: CMHCs carry the sector's most complex patients at the lowest effective rates — a combination that compresses clinician productivity and contributes to burnout. When rates are lower, clinicians must see more patients to generate the same revenue. When patients are higher-acuity, each visit takes more clinical time. The result: burnout that drives churn, which further constrains capacity.

Framing note: Problem 3 is an outcome of Problems 1 and 2. Each org feels the burnout cycle differently depending on their rate position and engagement pattern.

The Real Story

Centerstone's rates are nearly at best-in-class on the codes that matter. The three codes driving 93% of benchmarked revenue are all within 7% of P75 or above it. The transformation story here isn't about rates — it's about whether the extreme caseloads are sustainable and whether the volume model sacrifices per-patient depth.

The rate position is a strength to protect. The real questions are operational: can 6 therapists sustain 2,519 patients at best-in-class engagement levels? Can 3 treatment plan clinicians maintain quality at 3x the market standard? The answers to those questions determine whether Centerstone's volume model is a competitive advantage or a structural vulnerability.

Investigation Priorities

1. H2019 Therapy Panel & RPB Gap

419.8 benes/clinician is nearly double the best-in-class standard (227). Per-patient revenue ($163.92) may lag best-in-class levels. These two facts are likely connected — clinicians cannot engage patients deeply at this volume. Adding therapists could increase RPB toward best-in-class while reducing burnout risk.

Impact: High. Deeper engagement per patient across 2,519 benes, plus reduced burnout risk across 6 clinicians.

2. H0032 Treatment Plan Clinician Load

308 benes/clinician is 3x the best-in-class standard. The rate is strong ($86.52, above P75), so the risk is not financial — it is operational. At this throughput, what is the quality of treatment plans? What happens when one of the 3 clinicians is out?

Impact: Quality and retention. Treatment plan quality affects downstream engagement and outcomes.

3. 99213 Billing Configuration

A rate of $5.61 vs best-in-class $56.56 is anomalous. This is not a market gap — it is likely a billing configuration issue. With minimal revenue impact (0.2%), this is a quick-fix investigation: review modifier usage, place-of-service codes, and claim adjudication detail on recent 99213 submissions.

Impact: Small financially but signals possible systematic E&M billing issues.

4. Protect the H0040 ACT Position

ACT is your largest service line (~47.8% of revenue) with strong metrics: rate at $30.86 (within 1.7% of best-in-class), RPB at $756.44, 1,572 beneficiaries. The ACT position is a competitive strength. The risk is the 786 benes/clinician panel across only 2 clinicians — single-clinician departure would be catastrophic.

Impact: Existential. ~$1.19M in revenue concentrated in 2 clinicians. Succession planning essential.

The Volume-Over-Depth Question

Centerstone's core strategic question emerges from the intersection of Problems 2 and 3. Your therapy RPB of $163.92 may be below best-in-class levels. Against the CMHC sector median ($137.38), this looked like a +19% advantage. Against the best-in-class standard, it is a different story. This reframing matters because it changes the strategic direction:

  • Against the CMHC benchmark: You would conclude engagement is strong and focus elsewhere.
  • Against best-in-class: You see that 2,519 patients are being served broadly rather than deeply, and that investing in deeper per-patient engagement (more visits, longer courses of care) could generate more revenue without adding a single new patient.

The panel data (419.8 vs best-in-class 227.0) suggests the mechanism: clinicians at nearly 2x best-in-class capacity physically cannot provide best-in-class engagement depth. This is not a criticism — it is a resource allocation question with quantifiable upside.

Methodology

Data source: FL Medicaid FFS claims (DOGE/Medicaid Provider Spending), 2024. MCO encounter data not included.

Org identification: Claims matched by billing NPI to organizational entity via NPPES. Professional servicing NPIs only (entity_type_code = 1).

Peer groups: CMHC sector (86 orgs), Rest-of-FL Medicaid (excludes CMHCs), Same Classification, Look-alike peers.

Benchmark philosophy: This report uses a single benchmark: Rest-of-FL P75 — best-in-class. The CMHC sector median is shown in small gray text for context only. The rationale: the industry landscape report demonstrated that the CMHC sector systematically underperforms the broader market. Benchmarking against the sector normalizes that underperformance. P75 of the non-CMHC market represents what the top quartile achieves — the standard Centerstone should be measured against.

Metrics: Payment per claim (rate), revenue per beneficiary (engagement depth), claims per beneficiary (visit frequency), beneficiaries per clinician (panel size / productivity).

Sector context: Sector-level numbers ($8.1M rate gap, 23.6%→5.0% leakage, $157M upper-bound leakage gap) are from the FL CMHC Landscape Report v4, which benchmarks the full CMHC sector against all non-CMHC FL Medicaid providers.

Limitations: This report shows FL Medicaid FFS only — a partial view. Organization-level conclusions should be validated against internal data. MCO encounter data, commercial payers, grants, and facility-level billing are not captured. Beneficiary counts are not deduplicated across service codes. Revenue estimates for service lines are approximated from available claim and rate data.

This report maps your organization's position within the three structural challenges identified in the FL CMHC Landscape Report. It presents observations and investigation questions — it does not diagnose causes or prescribe actions. All numbers should be validated against internal claims data before operational decisions.
