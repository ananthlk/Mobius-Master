# Strategy Narrative Guide — CMHC Benchmarking

## Purpose
Translate benchmarking data into clear, client-facing strategic language for community mental health center (CMHC) strategy work. Findings should be actionable, not just descriptive.

---

## Narrative Structure

### For a Single Organization
1. **Opening context** — Who is this org, what markets do they operate in, what is their taxonomy profile?
2. **KPI summary** — How do they perform on each of the 3 KPIs vs. peers?
3. **Market context** — What archetype are their markets? Does that change the interpretation?
4. **Strategic implications** — What should leadership do with this information?
5. **Watch list** — Any outlier codes, markets, or time trends that warrant follow-up?

### For a Market Analysis
1. **Market overview** — FL landscape summary by MSA archetype
2. **Opportunity markets** — Where is demand high but service capacity low?
3. **Risk markets** — Where are providers potentially overextended or underreimbursed?
4. **Benchmark context** — How does FL compare to national norms on each KPI?
5. **Strategic priorities** — Top 3 market entry / expansion / contraction signals

---

## KPI Language Guide

### Panel Size
| Finding | Language |
|---------|----------|
| Low vs. peers | "The organization's patient reach is below peer norms, suggesting a marketing or referral pipeline gap rather than a capacity constraint." |
| High vs. peers | "Panel sizes are elevated relative to peers — this may reflect strong demand capture, but warrants a capacity and quality review to assess sustainability." |
| Trending up | "Panel size has grown consistently over the period, indicating successful patient acquisition or market expansion." |
| Trending down | "Declining panel size may signal patient attrition, referral disruption, or intentional panel right-sizing." |

### Claims per Beneficiary
| Finding | Language |
|---------|----------|
| Low vs. peers | "Claims per beneficiary are below peer norms — a potential indicator of elevated no-show rates or premature treatment discontinuation." |
| High vs. peers | "High visit frequency relative to peers may reflect a higher-acuity population, strong care engagement, or — at the extreme — warrants a utilization review." |
| Stable | "Visit frequency is consistent with peer norms and stable over time, suggesting predictable care patterns." |

### Payment per Claim
| Finding | Language |
|---------|----------|
| Low vs. peers | "Effective payment rates lag peer benchmarks. This may reflect payer mix differences, billing code selection, or contract terms that warrant renegotiation." |
| High vs. peers | "The organization achieves above-peer payment rates, likely reflecting favorable payer contracts, higher-complexity billing, or efficient coding practices." |
| Declining trend | "Payment per claim has declined over the period — monitor for payer policy changes or coding drift." |

## Market Archetype Language

| Archetype | Strategic Language |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Underserved / High Demand | "This market is supply-constrained. Existing providers carry outsized panel burdens relative to FL and national norms. New entrants or capacity expansion here would address a clear access gap." |
| Saturated / Competitive | "Provider density is high relative to available beneficiaries. Growth here requires differentiation on quality, payer relationships, or service scope — not simply adding capacity." |
| Underutilizing | "Despite reasonable provider availability, claims per beneficiary are low. This is a care engagement challenge — referral pipelines, no-show management, and community outreach are the primary levers." |
| High Intensity | "High utilization and strong payment rates characterize this market. It rewards providers with complex-care capabilities and strong billing infrastructure." |
| Low Reimbursement Trap | "This market has demand but poor reimbursement. Sustainability for providers here depends heavily on grant funding, FQHC designation, or Medicaid supplemental payments." |

## Framing Rules for Client Decks

1. **Lead with the implication, not the data.** Don't open with "Panel size is at the 34th percentile." Open with "This organization is under-reaching its market."
2. **Always contextualize.** A number without a benchmark is not insight.
3. **Be specific about geography.** "FL" is not a market. "Tampa MSA" is.
4. **Flag uncertainty.** DOGE data is Medicare FFS only — Medicaid, Medicare Advantage, and commercial are not represented. Note this limitation when panel size conclusions could be confounded by payer mix.
5. **Three strategic implications max per section.** Prioritize ruthlessly.

---

## Standard Caveats to Include

> "This analysis uses Medicare fee-for-service claims data from the DOGE dataset and reflects billing activity, not clinical outcomes. Panel size, utilization, and payment metrics are proxies — they should be interpreted alongside operational context from the organization's leadership."

> "Medicare FFS represents a subset of any provider's total patient population. Providers with high Medicaid or commercially-insured panels may appear smaller in this dataset than their true operational scale."
