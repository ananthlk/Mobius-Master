### The Right Benchmark: All FL Medicaid — Not Just Your Peers

This report uses **All FL Medicaid participants** as the primary benchmark. This is a deliberate choice, and understanding why it matters is the most important thing to take from this report.

Community mental health centers (CMHCs) tend to cluster tightly against each other. When compared only to similar CMHCs, an organization can appear "at market" or even "strong" — while sitting 20–30% below what the broader Medicaid system pays for equivalent services. That gap is not a rounding error. It reflects a structural underperformance of the CMHC sector as a whole.

**Why All FL Medicaid is the right comparison:**
- It includes FQHCs, RHCs, hospital outpatient departments, and independent practices billing the same codes — organizations that have structured their billing to perform at full Medicaid rates.
- It establishes what Medicaid is actually willing to pay for these services when billed optimally.
- In a period of heightened Medicaid scrutiny and potential rate compression, the organizations that will emerge strongest are those benchmarked against the full market, not just their sector peers.
- **Critically: Medicaid is the highest-paying payer for most CMHCs.** For FQHCs, hospitals, and independent practices in this comparison, Medicaid is typically one of their lower-paying payers — they also bill commercial insurance, Medicare, and self-pay at higher rates. A CMHC that is 20–30% below the All FL Medicaid median is not just underperforming against peers — it is underperforming against organizations for whom Medicaid is not even their primary revenue source. The gap should be zero, or close to it, because this is where CMHCs should have their strongest position.

**FQHCs as the aspirational marker:** Federally Qualified Health Centers bill at all-inclusive Prospective Payment System (PPS) encounter rates and consistently sit at or above the All FL Medicaid median for behavioral health codes. They are not a perfect comparator — their cost structure and population are different — but they represent what sustainable, high-performing Medicaid behavioral health billing looks like. Where the data permits, FQHC performance is called out as the upper bound of what is achievable in this market.

### The Rate Layer: Published Rate → Industry Paid Rate → Org Rate

Understanding the rate gap requires distinguishing between three numbers, not one:

| Layer | What it is | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **FL Medicaid Published Rate** | The fee schedule rate AHCA publishes per code — what Medicaid says it will pay | AHCA fee schedule |
| **All FL Medicaid avg paid rate** | What the industry actually collects per claim across all FL billers — reflects MCO contract rates, FFS/managed care mix, modifier usage, and payment adjustments | DOGE paid-claims data |
| **Org avg paid rate** | What your organization actually collects per claim | DOGE org-level data |

**Why this matters:** The gap from the published rate to the All FL Medicaid avg paid tells you how much the industry as a whole is leaving on the table due to MCO rate compression, contract terms, and billing variation. The gap from the All FL Medicaid avg paid to your org rate tells you your specific underperformance versus an already-compressed market. For CMHCs, both gaps are real — the sector collects below what the industry collects, and the industry collects below what the fee schedule promises.

**The full picture:** A code with a published rate of $75.00 where the All FL Medicaid avg paid is $62.00 and your org collects $49.00 tells a more complete story than simply "$13 below the state average." The $13 gap is yours to close. The $26 total gap from published to your rate is what optimal billing would capture — and it is what FQHCs and high-performing independent practices come closer to achieving.

In each code deep dive, the rate context table shows all three layers where published rate data is available.

**The CMHC sector gap is widening, not closing.** Across the codes analyzed in this report, the gap between CMHC-sector performance and All FL Medicaid has grown from 2019 to 2024. An organization that tracks against its CMHC peers and sees "stable" is, in absolute terms, falling further behind the broader market. This report names that gap directly.

**Goal:** The standard in this report is not "above your peers." It is "closing the gap to All FL Medicaid" — and ultimately positioning above it. That is the benchmark that survives rate pressure, policy change, and the next scrutiny cycle.

### Look-Alike Peers: Context, Not Target

Your organization is also compared against 10 look-alike peers selected by similarity of Medicaid revenue mix (similarity scores 0.7639–0.8559). Look-alike comparisons are included for context — they show where you stand within the CMHC band. But the band itself is the problem. Look-alike position tells you how you are doing relative to organizations facing the same structural constraints. All FL Medicaid position tells you how far those constraints are costing you.

### How to Read the Charts

In each chart, the diamond marks your organization's value. The **blue line** marks the All FL Medicaid median — the primary benchmark. The **purple line** marks the look-alike peer median. The **gray band** spans P25 to P75 of All FL Medicaid. When your diamond sits above the blue line, you are outperforming the full market. When it sits below, you are in the sector gap.

### Badge Legend

| Badge | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Above Industry** | Above the All FL Medicaid median — outperforming the full market |
| **At Industry** | Near the All FL Medicaid median — holding market position |
| **Sector Gap** | Below All FL Medicaid median but at or above CMHC peers — performing within sector but the sector is behind |
| **Falling Behind** | Below both All FL Medicaid and CMHC medians, and/or trend is diverging downward |

### Key Limitation

Rate per claim measures what your organization is paid per claim submission, not per 15-minute unit of service. Organizations that bill longer sessions will show higher per-claim amounts. FQHCs billing at PPS encounter rates will show structurally higher per-claim amounts — this is a billing model difference, not a negotiation outcome, and is noted where relevant.

### How We Frame Outliers

When a metric falls significantly below the All FL Medicaid median, this report does not speculate on the cause. It frames the gap as a question for your team: is this a rate issue, a billing structure issue, a caseload issue, or a service model difference? The goal is to name the gap clearly so the investigation can begin — not to assign blame or draw conclusions from claims data alone.

## Section 5: Caveats and Methodology Notes

### Peer Methodology

The look-alike methodology matches organizations by the similarity of their visible Medicaid revenue mix. It identifies peers who bill a comparable blend of outpatient therapy, ACT, and case management codes. However, it does not directly match on organizational structure. Your organization operates a crisis stabilization unit (23.1% of visible revenue) and is registered as a residential treatment facility, but most look-alike peers lack these service lines. For crisis and residential services, a service-specific peer group would provide more meaningful comparison.

### Rate per Claim vs Rate per Unit

All rate-per-claim figures represent the average payment per claim submission. For time-based codes, a single claim may encompass multiple 15-minute units.

### Modifier Rollup

Assessment codes (H0031, H0032) combine all modifier variations into a single per-code figure.

### Residential Gap

Your organization is registered as a residential treatment facility, but no residential per-diem codes appear in this data, so residential revenue is entirely absent from this benchmarking.

### Volume Limitations

Three codes have fewer than 100 claims -- H0031 (91 claims), H0032 (68 claims), and 99214 (45 claims) -- and metrics for these codes carry greater uncertainty due to small sample sizes.
