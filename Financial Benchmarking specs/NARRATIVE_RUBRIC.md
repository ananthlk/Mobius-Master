# Narrative Rubric — Financial Benchmarking Report

## Purpose
Evaluate each section of the financial benchmarking narrative before inclusion in the report. Score 1-5 on each criterion. Minimum 4.0 average to pass.

## Criteria

### 1. Factual Accuracy (weight: 30%)
- Numbers cited match the underlying data exactly
- Percentile positions (WELL ABOVE, Below, etc.) are correct
- Trend signals match the computed direction
- No invented or hallucinated statistics
- Peer group N is stated when citing benchmarks

### 2. Caveat Discipline (weight: 20%)
- Unit-of-service limitation stated when discussing per-claim rates for 15-min codes
- Modifier rollup noted when relevant (H0031, H0032)
- FQHC/RHC PPS exception noted if comparing those org types
- "Question not conclusion" framing — observations lead to investigation prompts, not diagnoses
- MCO vs FFS rate variation acknowledged

### 3. Client Language (weight: 15%)
- No taxonomy codes in body text (use descriptive names)
- No internal model names (org_entity_id, peer_group, dimension_value)
- "Your organization" not "DLC" or org name (report should be reusable)
- Peer groups described in plain English ("organizations of similar size" not "by_size:medium")
- Medical/billing terms defined on first use or avoided

### 4. Insight Quality (weight: 20%)
- Each finding leads with the **All FL Medicaid gap** — the CMHC peer comparison is secondary context, not the headline
- The narrative distinguishes between "above your peers but below the market" (Sector Gap) and "above the market" (Above Industry)
- **FQHC/hospital PPS context** is noted where relevant: when All FL Medicaid includes PPS billers, the narrative should acknowledge this structural difference without using it to excuse the gap
- The **Medicaid-as-ceiling point** is reinforced: for CMHCs, Medicaid is typically the highest-paying payer; being below the All FL Medicaid median means underperforming against organizations for whom Medicaid is a lower-tier payer
- Trend direction names the direction vs All FL Medicaid, not just vs CMHC peers — "stable vs peers but falling behind the market" is a distinct and important signal
- Each finding has a "so what" framed around the gap to industry, not the gap to sector
- Flags where further investigation is needed (not just "this is high/low")

### 4a. Sector Gap Framing (mandatory)
- The report must establish early (Executive Summary or Section 1) that the CMHC sector as a whole is 20–30% below All FL Medicaid on key metrics
- Individual org performance must then be framed in that context: "you are at/above your peers" is meaningless if the peer group is itself underperforming
- The aspirational goal stated in the report must be: closing the gap to All FL Medicaid median, and ultimately positioning above it
- **Automatic fail:** Any section that presents look-alike peer performance as the primary benchmark without first establishing the sector gap to All FL Medicaid

### 4b. Rate Layer Framing (when rate data is present)
- Rate discussions must distinguish between **three layers**, not one:
  1. **FL Medicaid published rate** — what AHCA's fee schedule says Medicaid will pay (the ceiling)
  2. **All FL Medicaid avg paid rate** — what the industry actually collects (compressed by MCO contracts, FFS/managed care mix, modifier patterns)
  3. **Org avg paid rate** — what this organization actually collects
- When the published rate is available for a code, both gaps must be named: (a) industry-to-published (structural compression the whole market faces) and (b) org-to-industry (this org's specific underperformance)
- The published rate is not presented as easily achievable — the narrative should explain that the all-FL paid rate already reflects industry-wide compression, so the org-to-industry gap is the most actionable
- If the published rate is not available for a given code, the narrative should still use the published/paid/org framing conceptually, noting the published rate will be added when fee schedule data is available
- **Automatic fail:** Any rate section that discusses only "org vs state average" without contextualizing where the state average itself sits relative to the published fee schedule

### 5. Brevity & Structure (weight: 15%)
- One paragraph per code (3-5 sentences max)
- Lead with the finding, not the methodology
- Service line header provides context before code-level detail
- No redundant restating of what the chart shows — the narrative adds interpretation
- Executive summary is 3-5 bullet points, each one sentence

## Scoring Guide

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 5 | Publication-ready, no changes needed |
| 4 | Minor polish needed, factually sound |
| 3 | Needs revision — missing caveats, unclear language, or weak insights |
| 2 | Significant issues — factual errors, jargon, or unsupported claims |
| 1 | Rewrite needed |

## Disqualifiers (automatic fail regardless of score)
- Any factual error in cited numbers
- Stating a conclusion without caveat where data limitations apply
- Using internal/technical terminology in client-facing text
- Making a recommendation (report observes and questions, does not prescribe)
