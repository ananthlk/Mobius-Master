1. Executive Summary

Three forces shape CMHC financial performance in Florida Medicaid. All three are measurable. They are not equal in impact.

  • Problem A — The rate gap is real: $8.1M. On intake and high-acuity services, CMHCs earn less per beneficiary than the rest of the FL Medicaid market. Intake: $74.96 vs $88.18 per bene (-15%). High-acuity stabilization: $908 vs $1,217 per bene (-25.4%). On ongoing care, CMHCs actually earn more per beneficiary (+$43.78). The net rate loss across the sector's current patient volume is $8.1M.
  • Problem B — The clinician productivity burden is real. CMHC clinicians carry substantially smaller panels than the rest of the market on the highest-volume codes. H2019 (behavioral therapy): 123 benes/clinician vs 227 (-45.8%). 90837 (psychotherapy 60min): 26 vs 97.5 (-73.3%). H0040 (ACT): 345 vs 618 (-44.1%). At the same time, CMHC intake clinicians carry larger panels than the market — H0031: 115 vs 76.5 benes/clinician (+50.9%). The pattern: high throughput at the front door, lower capacity downstream. Whether this reflects acuity differences, staffing constraints, or model design, it compresses revenue per clinician on the services that generate recurring income.
  • Problem C — The leakage problem dwarfs both: $157M (upper bound). CMHCs handle 23.6% of all FL Medicaid BH intake. But the sector's share of beneficiaries drops to 14.4% for high-acuity stabilization and 5.0% for ongoing care. If CMHCs retained their intake-level market share through ongoing care, the sector would generate $183M in ongoing revenue instead of $38.6M. The leakage gap — up to $157M — is 19x the rate gap. (Note: These are market share figures by service category, not tracked patient transitions. The true retention rate requires patient-level claims linkage. The $157M assumes full capacity to retain — actual recoverable leakage will be lower, but even a fraction is transformative.)

A surprising finding: In ongoing care, CMHCs actually earn more per beneficiary than the rest of the market — $133.69 vs $89.91 (+48.7%). When patients stay, CMHCs deliver more intensive, higher-value care per person. This may reflect a selection effect (only the highest-acuity patients remain), a different code mix (more H2019 therapy, less low-yield E/M), or genuinely more intensive engagement. Regardless, it means the sector doesn't have an ongoing care rate problem — it has an ongoing care volume problem.

The implication: Closing every rate gap would add $8.1M. Important, but incremental. Recovering even a fraction of the leakage — moving ongoing care share from 5% to 10% — would add approximately $38.6M at current per-beneficiary rates. The strategic question isn't just "how do we get paid more per visit?" but "how do we keep patients in care after the front door?"

The pattern: CMHC clinicians run high-throughput intake (50% more assessments per person) and high-caseload case management (+8.2%). But on the revenue-generating ongoing-care services — therapy, PSR, psychotherapy — panels are 34–73% smaller than the market.

What to investigate:

  • Is the therapy panel gap a capacity issue or an acuity issue? If CMHC patients are higher-acuity and need more time per session, smaller panels may be clinically appropriate. If it reflects scheduling gaps, no-shows, or clinician turnover, it's addressable.
  • Is the large case management caseload sustainable? At 275 benes/clinician (+8.2% above market), CMHC case managers may be stretched thin — which could partly explain the shorter session lengths on T1017 (3.3 vs 4.3 units/claim).
  • Where are clinicians spending time that doesn't show up in claims? If CMHC clinicians carry smaller billable panels because of documentation burden, no-shows, or unfunded care coordination, the productivity gap is real but the fix isn't "see more patients" — it's reducing non-billable time.

4. Problem C: The Leakage — $157M

This is the largest finding in the data. The CMHC sector's share of FL Medicaid BH beneficiaries drops nearly 5x from intake-category services to ongoing-care services.

Important caveat: These percentages reflect the sector's share of beneficiaries within each service category, not tracked patient journeys. A patient receiving both intake (H0031) and therapy (H2019) at the same provider appears in both segments. The 23.6% → 5.0% drop is a market share fact — it shows the sector is disproportionately concentrated in intake and underrepresented in ongoing care — but the true patient retention rate requires beneficiary-level linkage across providers, which this dataset does not support.

The Surprising Finding: CMHCs Earn More Per Bene in Ongoing Care (+48.7%)

In ongoing care — the segment where CMHCs have the smallest market share — the sector earns $133.69 per beneficiary vs $89.91 for Rest of FL. This is the report's most striking number. Three possible explanations:

  • Selection effect: Only the highest-acuity patients remain at CMHCs for ongoing care, requiring more intensive (and higher-billed) services per person.
  • Code mix: CMHC ongoing care skews toward H2019 ($74.53/claim) and T1017 ($49.58/claim) rather than low-yield 99213 visits ($30.38). The rest of the market has 2.4M 99213 claims diluting their per-bene average.
  • Engagement depth: The patients who stay at CMHCs get more visits per person, generating higher per-bene revenue even if per-claim rates are similar.

The strategic implication: CMHCs are good at ongoing care when they have patients. The per-bene rate is not the problem — the volume is. This makes the leakage gap even more consequential: every retained patient generates above-market revenue.

5. Questions for CMHC Leadership

A. The Rate Gaps

T1017: Are your case management sessions shorter than 65 minutes by design? Compare your units/claim to the 4.3 market norm. If sessions are compressed by scheduling pressure, this is addressable.

90837: Is 60-minute psychotherapy a core service line, or incidental? If the panel is under 30, the rate gap matters less than the question of whether to offer the service at all.

99213 vs 99214: Review your E/M visit mix. A shift from L3 to L4 where documentation supports it changes the revenue picture.

B. Clinician Burden

Therapy panels at CMHCs are 46% smaller than the market (123 vs 227 benes/clinician on H2019). Is this because CMHC patients are higher-acuity and need more time per session? Or because of scheduling gaps, no-shows, and unfunded care coordination?

Case management panels are 8% larger than market (275 vs 254). Combined with shorter sessions (3.3 vs 4.3 units), this may signal burnout or time compression.

C. The Leakage

Map your own intake-to-ongoing-care conversion rate. How many patients assessed at your organization return for a first therapy or medication visit within 30 days? Within 90 days?

Where are they going? If patients leave because you don't offer medication management on-site, that's a service expansion question. If they leave because the wait for a first appointment is too long, that's a capacity question.

D. The Opportunity

Moving from 5% to 10% ongoing care market share — still less than half the sector's intake share — would roughly double CMHC ongoing revenue statewide.

The ongoing care per-bene rate is already strong ($133.69 vs $89.91 for Rest of FL). The sector doesn't need better rates on these services. It needs more patients staying.

6. Service Line Deep Dives

6.1 H2019 — Behavioral Therapy Services

Rate: CMHC P50 $74.53 vs Rest-of-FL $70.26 (+6.1%). Implied units: 3.4 vs 3.2 — session lengths comparable, rate advantage is real.

Panel: 123 vs 227 benes/clinician (-45.8%). Engagement: 2.04 vs 2.59 claims/bene (-21.2%). Revenue/bene: $137.38 vs $185.98 (-26.1%).

Question: The sector bills at a higher per-claim rate with comparable session length — why doesn't that translate to stronger revenue per patient? Fewer visits per patient and half the panel size erode the rate advantage. Is the smaller panel an acuity difference or a capacity constraint?

6.2 T1017 — Targeted Case Management

Rate: CMHC P50 $49.58 vs Rest-of-FL $63.45 (-21.9%). Implied units: 3.3 vs 4.3 — CMHCs bill 23% shorter sessions.

Panel: 275 vs 254 benes/clinician (+8.2%). Engagement: 4.70 vs 5.63 claims/bene (-16.5%). Revenue/bene: $240.24 vs $370.51 (-35.2%).

Question: Larger caseloads + shorter sessions + fewer touches per patient = the operational signature of stretched case management. Is this the right workload, or are clinicians under capacity pressure?

6.3 H2017 — Psychosocial Rehabilitation

Rate: CMHC P50 $123.82 vs Rest-of-FL $120.28 (+2.9%). Implied units: 13.6 vs 13.2 — nearly identical ~3.4 hour sessions.

Panel: 172.5 vs 260 (-33.7%). Engagement: 11.80 vs 11.64 (+1.4%). Revenue/bene: $1,342.29 vs $1,379.46 (-2.7%).

Question: Near parity on rate, engagement, and revenue per bene. This is the sector's strongest ongoing-care code. What makes PSR delivery work where other codes show gaps? Is it the group format, the staffing model, or the contract structure?

6.4 T1015 — Medication Management

Rate: CMHC P50 $65.02 vs Rest-of-FL $70.86 (-8.2%). Published rate: $71.61/event. Per-event code — gap is purely contract/modifier.

Panel & Engagement: At parity (~224 benes, ~1.1 claims/bene).

Question: Are CMHC contracts discounting T1015 below the fee schedule? And critically — given that CMHCs have only 1% market share in medication management, is the bigger question whether to expand this service line rather than optimize its rate?

6.5 H0040 — Assertive Community Treatment (FACT)

Rate: CMHC P50 $30.30 vs Rest-of-FL $30.90 (-1.9%). Published rate: $31.55/diem. At parity.

Engagement: 23.60 vs 17.52 claims/bene (+34.7%). Revenue/bene: $721.37 vs $552.69 (+30.5%).

Question: FACT is a success story — highest engagement intensity in the dataset, highest revenue per bene among ongoing codes. Only 6 CMHCs bill it. Is this model scalable?

6.6 Assessment Codes (H0031, H0032, H2000)

H0031: At parity (+0.4%), CMHC panel 50.9% larger than market. H0032: At parity (+0.6%). H2000: +17.6% above market, suggesting physician-led evaluations.

Question: Front-door rates are competitive. The sector does more intake per clinician than anyone. Is this sustainable, and how does it connect to the downstream leakage?

6.7 E/M and Psychotherapy Codes

99214: +21.1% above market — the sector's strongest E/M code. 90833 (add-on): +25.8% but applied to far fewer patients (43 vs 140 panel). 90837 (60min): -34.6% — steepest gap, smallest panel (26 benes).

Question: Is 90833 underutilized? If prescribers conduct therapy components during medication visits, billing the add-on captures value already delivered. Is 90837 worth investing in or should the sector focus on H2019 where it has a rate advantage?

7. Methodology and Caveats

Data source: Florida Medicaid Fee-for-Service claims (DOGE/Medicaid Provider Spending), 2024 calendar year. Managed care encounter data is not included.

Comparison group: "Rest of FL" = all non-CMHC providers billing these codes in FL Medicaid FFS. Includes FQHCs (PPS rates may elevate some medians), hospitals, private practices, and other provider types.

Three care segments: Intake (H0031, H0032, H2000, 90791, 90792), High-Acuity (H2017, H0040, H0036, H2010), Ongoing (H2019, T1017, T1015, 99213/14/15/12, 90832/33/34/37, H0004, H0048, T1007, 99203/04). Assignment is by HCPCS code, not by patient journey — the same patient may appear in multiple segments.

Market share calculation: CMHC beneficiaries as % of total beneficiaries in each segment. Beneficiary counts are not deduplicated across segments or across providers within a segment.

Rate gap calculation: (Rest-of-FL revenue per bene - CMHC revenue per bene) x CMHC beneficiary count, summed across segments where CMHC earns less. Ongoing care, where CMHC earns more per bene, is excluded from the rate loss total.

Leakage gap calculation: If CMHC held 23.6% (its intake share) of ongoing care beneficiaries instead of 5.0%, at CMHC's own per-bene rate. This is an upper bound — actual recoverable leakage depends on service capacity, patient preferences, and geographic access.

Median (P50) as benchmark: All per-claim comparisons use the median across organizations. Provider count (N) varies by code — small N (under 10) limits reliability.

What this report does not include: Individual organization positioning, trend data, MCO contract comparisons, quality/outcome measures, cost-to-deliver analysis, or geographic variation within Florida.

This report presents sector-level observations and investigation questions. It does not diagnose causes or prescribe actions. All numbers are from the canonical dataset and should be validated against source claims before operational decisions.
